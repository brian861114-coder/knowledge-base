## Why it matters

If dislocations did not exist, crystalline metals would be far stronger and far less useful. Real plasticity in most crystals is a dislocation problem.

## Core idea

A dislocation is a one-dimensional defect representing a mismatch in lattice registry. The two idealized limits are edge and screw dislocations, though real dislocations often have mixed character. Under applied stress, dislocations can glide or climb, allowing permanent deformation without forcing an entire atomic plane to move at once.

That mechanism explains why crystals deform plastically at stresses much lower than the theoretical strength predicted for a defect-free lattice. It also explains why barriers to dislocation motion raise strength.

## Key examples

FCC metals typically have many active slip systems and therefore deform readily. HCP systems often have more limited slip at room temperature, which can make ductility more orientation-dependent. Work hardening is a classic consequence of rising dislocation density during deformation.

## Common mistakes

Do not say "defects are bad" as if dislocations are only damage. They are also the enablers of useful plasticity. Also, dislocations are not directly visible in ordinary optical microscopy; invoking them without a structural mechanism or evidence is easy, but not rigorous.
