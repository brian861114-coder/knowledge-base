## Why it matters

Grain boundaries are not passive separators. They are active structural features that can strengthen, weaken, accelerate transport, trap impurities, or redirect cracks depending on context.

## Core idea

Because adjacent grains do not align perfectly, the interface stores disorder and energy. That changes how defects move, how corrosion localizes, and how a material responds to deformation and heat.

## Links

[[Strengthening Mechanisms]] uses boundary density as a classic strength lever. [[Creep Mechanisms]] and [[Corrosion]] both care about grain-boundary transport and chemical sensitivity. [[Electron Backscatter Diffraction]] is one of the best tools for mapping grain-scale structure directly.

## Key examples

Fine grains can increase strength by making dislocation motion more difficult, but the same increased boundary area can accelerate diffusion-dominated processes or localized attack in some environments. Special boundaries such as coherent twin boundaries can behave differently from random high-angle boundaries.

## Common mistakes

Do not treat every grain boundary as equivalent. Boundary character matters, not just boundary count. Also, more boundaries are not automatically beneficial; the effect depends on temperature, environment, deformation mode, and property of interest.
