## Why it matters

Knowing which phases exist is not enough. Their amount, size, connectivity, and shape often matter just as much.

## Core idea

Phase fraction tells you the relative amount of each phase in the microstructure. Morphology tells you whether those phases are lamellar, globular, acicular, dendritic, precipitate-like, continuous, or isolated. The same chemistry can produce very different performance when phase topology changes.

A hard phase that is fine and dispersed behaves differently from the same phase when coarse and continuous. A corrosive path through a connected phase network differs from isolated particles of the same chemistry.

## Links

[[Precipitation Hardening]] is a direct example where fraction and morphology control strengthening efficiency. [[Mechanical Properties]] and [[Corrosion]] often depend on whether a phase is continuous, brittle, galvanically active, or crack-deflecting.

## Key examples

Fine precipitates strengthen more effectively than coarse overaged ones in many alloy systems. Lamellar or plate-like morphologies can improve some properties while worsening crack sensitivity in others.

## Common mistakes

Do not reduce microstructure to phase labels only. "Contains phase X" is weak information unless you also understand how much of it exists and how it is arranged.
