## Why it matters

Point defects look small only in geometry. In kinetics, transport, and functional materials, they are often decisive.

## Core idea

A vacancy is a missing atom on an expected lattice site. An interstitial is an extra atom occupying a normally unfilled position. A substitutional defect replaces a host atom with a different species. Each defect changes local bonding, elastic strain, and sometimes charge balance.

Because point defects can be thermally generated, compositionally introduced, or driven by atmosphere and electrochemical conditions, they are central to diffusion, nonstoichiometry, ionic transport, and defect-mediated reactions.

## Key examples

Vacancies enable substitutional diffusion in many metals. Oxygen vacancies are critical in many oxides and solid electrolytes. Carbon in interstitial positions is a classic example of how a small atom can strongly alter steel behavior.

## Common mistakes

Do not treat all defect concentrations as accidental contamination. Many useful materials are intentionally engineered around defect populations. Also, do not assume a higher defect concentration is always beneficial; it can improve transport while also reducing stability or increasing leakage.
