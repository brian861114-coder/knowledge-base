## Why it matters

Without symmetry and unit-cell language, crystal structure becomes a pile of pictures. With it, you can describe orientation, equivalent directions, slip families, and diffraction conditions precisely instead of vaguely.

## Core idea

A unit cell is the smallest repeating volume that can reproduce the periodic structure through translation. Symmetry operations such as rotation, reflection, inversion, and translation classify which directions or planes are equivalent and which physical responses may differ by orientation.

In materials science this matters immediately. Close-packed directions, slip planes, cleavage tendencies, and diffraction peaks all depend on crystallographic geometry, not just on chemical composition.

## Key examples

In cubic systems many directions are symmetry-equivalent, which simplifies indexing and often reduces anisotropy relative to lower-symmetry systems. In hexagonal systems the directional inequivalence is stronger, so slip behavior and elastic response can become much more orientation-sensitive.

## Common mistakes

Do not confuse "unit cell" with "grain" or "phase." A grain is a crystallographically coherent region in a real material; a unit cell is the mathematical repeat building block inside that grain. Also, high symmetry does not mean isotropy in every real specimen, because texture can reintroduce directional bias.
